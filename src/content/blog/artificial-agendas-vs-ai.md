While the global technology ecosystem is busy building autonomous futures, our parliament is trapped in a loop of debating artificial agendas. The disconnect between what is technically possible today and what our lawmakers are focusing on is staggering.

Look at our state operations. We have a massive, bloated bureaucracy with an overwhelming number of civil servants, yet simple administrative tasks take entirely too long. Our judicial system is notoriously slow; a standard court case can drag on for years, burying judges and citizens alike under mountains of paperwork. We log into e-Devlet to download a PDF, but the system does nothing to actively guide us or automate our interactions with the state.

Why aren't we using AI to pre-process legal casework and summarize documents for judges to speed up the courts? Why isn't autonomous software handling routine bureaucratic approvals, allowing the state to operate faster and leaner?

The answer is simple: awareness and authority are completely misaligned. The political class is entirely consumed by manufactured daily controversies — artificial agendas designed to distract. They do not understand the capabilities of modern AI, nor do they seem to care. Meanwhile, those who actually understand how to optimize these broken systems hold no authority to execute them. Until our lawmakers realize that the future of a strong state relies on technological efficiency rather than bureaucratic bloat, we will remain stuck in the past.
